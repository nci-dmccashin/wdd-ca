Recycling Information Centre

Plastic Pollution

Plastic pollution happens when plastic items end up in the environment instead of being reused or recycled. Because plastic takes hundreds of years to break down, it builds up in oceans, rivers and on land. This harms wildlife, damages ecosystems and contributes to long-term environmental stress. The problem keeps growing as we produce more single-use plastics than we can properly manage.

Litter

Litter is any waste that’s thrown or left in public places instead of being disposed of correctly. Even small bits — like wrappers, bottles or cigarette butts — can add up and cause big environmental damage. Litter makes communities look neglected, pollutes waterways, and can harm animals who mistake it for food. It’s a simple problem to fix, but it relies on everyone choosing to dispose of waste responsibly.

Health Impacts

Improper waste management and plastic pollution can lead to real health risks. Chemicals from plastics can leach into soil and water, potentially entering the food chain. Microplastics are now found in drinking water and even the air, and while research is ongoing, early studies suggest they may contribute to respiratory issues and other long-term health concerns. Littered areas also attract pests and bacteria, which can increase the spread of disease.

The Return Scheme

A return scheme encourages people to bring back bottles or cans in exchange for a small refund. This system reduces waste by making recycling easier and giving people a reason to participate. It keeps plastic out of the environment, cuts down on litter, and helps manufacturers reuse materials instead of producing new plastic. Over time, return schemes can significantly reduce pollution and support a more sustainable circular economy.

Global Warming

Global warming is causing noticeable changes to the planet’s climate, and its effects are becoming more severe each year. Rising temperatures are leading to more frequent heatwaves, stronger storms and unpredictable weather patterns. Ice caps and glaciers are melting, which raises sea levels and increases the risk of flooding in coastal areas. These changes put pressure on wildlife, damage ecosystems, and affect human life by threatening food supplies, water resources and infrastructure. If global warming continues unchecked, the long-term consequences will be far more difficult and expensive to reverse.
